## POST

The defense IME comes back bad. The reflex is to buy a rebuttal. That reflex is why small firms spend 4 figures on reports that never change a number.

A rebuttal is worth ordering when it changes what the carrier believes it can prove. Most of the time the IME already told you it won't.

Read the report for what it actually did. A large share of defense IMEs don't dispute the injury at all. They concede the diagnosis and attack duration, or apportion to degenerative findings, or say treatment was excessive past a date. Those are damages arguments dressed as medicine, and you beat them with the treating provider's records and a competent cross, not with a second opinion that costs $3,500 and arrives 6 weeks later.

The IME worth rebutting is the one that denies causation outright, or assigns a permanent impairment rating materially below your treating provider's, or introduces a mechanism-of-injury opinion your file has no answer to. Those change the ceiling. The rest change the argument.

There's a cheaper move that gets skipped constantly. Before buying anything, send the IME report to the treating provider and ask for a written response. They already know the patient, they've already been paid, and a 2-paragraph letter from the treater frequently does the entire job. Firms skip this because the treater is slow, then spend $3,500 to avoid a phone call.

> **Order a paid rebuttal only when all 3 are true:**
>
> 1. The IME attacks causation, permanency, or mechanism, rather than duration or reasonableness of treatment
> 2. The treating provider has already been asked for a written response and either declined, or responded in a way that doesn't cover the disputed point
> 3. The case value at stake exceeds the rebuttal cost by a defensible multiple, and the file is on a litigation track where the report will actually be used
>
> If any one is false, the answer is the treater letter plus cross-examination.
>
> **Timing when you do order:** immediately on receipt, never at the discovery deadline. A rebuttal that arrives after the carrier has re-reserved on the IME is arguing with a decision that's already priced.
>
> **Exception:** your jurisdiction's disclosure deadline forces the order earlier than this rule allows. The deadline wins. Note it in the file so the decision reads as a deadline call and not as an exception to the standard.

Condition 2 is the one that saves real money, and it's a paralegal task.

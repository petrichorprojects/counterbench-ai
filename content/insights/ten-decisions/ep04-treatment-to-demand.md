## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

A 30-day gap in treatment is a red flag on nearly every carrier's screen. Most PI firms find out which of their files have one at demand time, which is roughly 4 months too late to do anything about it.

The gap is the most expensive thing on a soft-tissue file and it costs nothing to prevent.

Here's how carriers actually read the treatment calendar. First visit inside 3 days of the loss builds a clean causation link that's hard to attack. Push the first visit past a week and many carriers apply an internal discount before a human has read anything. A 1 to 2 week gap mid-treatment raises a question you can usually answer with a clinic cancellation record or a preauthorization delay. Past 30 days it stops being a question and becomes the defense theory: the injury resolved, and whatever happened later came from somewhere else.

None of that is a judgment call about your client's credibility. It's a scoring pattern, applied early, often before anyone at the carrier has looked at a single record.

Which changes what your firm should be watching. The number that matters is days since last treatment, tracked on every open file, every week. The demand date takes care of itself once that one is under control.

> **Demand goes out when all 3 are true:**
>
> 1. The client is at MMI, discharged, or documented as plateaued by a treating provider
> 2. All records and bills are in hand, per the rolling waves in decision 3
> 3. Liens are identified and in process, per decision 7
>
> **Separately, and this is the part that actually protects the number:** every open file gets a days-since-last-treatment check weekly. At 21 days with no scheduled visit, someone calls the client. The purpose is to find out why they stopped: because they're better, because they can't afford it, because the provider cancelled, or because they forgot. Pushing treatment is a separate question and it's the attorney's call, not the paralegal's, and not the point of this call.
>
> Whatever the answer is, it gets documented that week, contemporaneously. A contemporaneous note explaining a gap is worth 10x the same explanation reconstructed at demand time.
>
> **Exception:** the client is better and done at 3 weeks with a clean discharge. That's not a gap, it's a resolved case. Demand goes out early and the speed is an asset.

The 21-day call is the whole post. It's a paralegal task, it takes 4 minutes, and it's the difference between a documented interruption and a hole in the story.

That's decision 4 of 10.

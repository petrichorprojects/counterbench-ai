## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

Ask 3 people at a small PI firm how the demand number gets set and you'll get "the attorney decides," which is a description of who types it, not of how it's derived.

That distinction shows up later, when the adjuster asks what the number is built from and the answer has to be assembled on the phone.

A number you can't decompose is a number you'll negotiate against yourself. The adjuster knows it inside 2 minutes. They start pulling components, you defend the total instead of the components, and every concession comes off a figure nobody can source.

Multipliers are where this goes wrong most often. A firm settles into 3x meds as a habit, applies it to a file with 40% chiropractic billing, and hands the carrier a number they can attack by attacking the denominator. The multiplier was never the argument. It was a shortcut standing in for one.

Here's what a decomposable number looks like, and it's the same 5 lines every time.

> **Every demand number is built from named components, written down before the demand is drafted:**
>
> 1. **Medical specials**, split into billed and reasonable-value where they diverge, with the write-off and lien picture noted
> 2. **Wage loss**, documented by employer, with the calculation shown
> 3. **Future care**, only when a provider has put it in writing. No projections the file can't support.
> 4. **General damages**, anchored to something external: verdict and settlement data for the venue, injury type, and treatment course. Never a bare multiple of specials.
> 5. **The policy and liability reality**: limits, comparative fault exposure, collectibility
>
> **Who sets it:** the attorney sets general damages and the final number. Everything else is assembled by staff and reviewed. Line 4 is the only line requiring judgment, and it's the only line an attorney should be spending time on.
>
> **The gate:** the number doesn't leave the building until someone who didn't build it can read the sheet and reconstruct the total. If they can't, the demand isn't ready.
>
> **Exception:** limits are clear and the case is obviously worth them. Then the number is the limits, the sheet exists anyway, and it exists to support the bad-faith setup rather than the negotiation.

The component sheet takes 20 minutes and it survives the case. When the file moves to litigation 8 months later, the reasoning is on paper instead of in whoever handled it.

## Notes

**The multiplier critique is the risky part and the reason to publish it.** Plenty of firms run on a multiple of specials. Saying it's a shortcut standing in for an argument is a real position, and the chiropractic-denominator example makes it concrete rather than snobby.

**"Someone who didn't build it can reconstruct the total" is the shippable test.** It's binary, it takes 5 minutes, and it maps directly onto the series thesis about knowledge leaving one person's head.

**Verification status.** Entirely constructed. This is the episode where PWB review matters most, because the component list is effectively a claim about how PI damages should be built. Ask specifically whether line 1's billed vs reasonable-value split matches their jurisdiction's collateral source treatment.

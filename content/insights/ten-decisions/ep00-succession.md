## POST

The Managing Partner Forum puts it at 70%. Seven in 10 first-generation law firms don't survive their founding partners.

As of the last Thomson Reuters survey on it, in 2018, 37% of firms had a succession plan or were building one.

So start with the count. How many processes at your firm can only one person run?

That number is your succession plan. You didn't write it. It's accurate anyway.

Here's what set me off.

A partner announced he's retiring in 2 years. Then he told a second-year associate to first chair the next trial. The associate has never watched a trial start to finish. 3 came close and all 3 settled on the courthouse steps.

The thread called it reckless. It reads to me like a firm doing arithmetic in public.

The partner is already gone mentally. Everything he knows about picking a jury, reading a room, and recognizing when the number in front of him is the best number he's getting sits in his head with a 2-year fuse on it. The trial date is just where the fuse first becomes visible.

Small firms have a specific version of this problem.

The knowledge lives in the gap between what a lawyer did and why they did it. Your case management system records the what. Your closed files record the what. The why has never been written down once.

Most firms respond by buying time. Extend the retirement. Hire a lateral. Assign a mentor.

Time by itself moves nothing. A mentor with a 2-year fuse produces an associate who watched.

The transfer happens when the decisions leave the head and enter the firm. In order of cheapest first:

**1. Record the why.** 20 minutes on video after any significant call or ruling. Skip the recap. Say what you were choosing between and why you landed where you did. 10 of those beat 100 closed files.

**2. Write down the decisions that repeat.** Skip the exotic calls. Document the ones that happen on every single file. At a PI firm there are about 10 of them.

**3. Name a backup for every recurring process, then make them run it.** An untested backup is a name on a chart.

**4. Start second chair rotations 3 years out.** Three months after the retirement announcement is theater.

None of this needs a consultant, a platform, or a budget line. It needs someone to decide that one person's memory is an unacceptable place to store a law firm.

Starting next week I'm publishing those 10 decisions, one a week, each written so a paralegal can execute it without asking anyone.

Go do the count first. If the number is above 3, you're carrying exposure you haven't priced.

If you'd rather have all 10 as one document than wait 10 weeks, message me and I'll send the working draft.

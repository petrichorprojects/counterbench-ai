## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

Records are the single largest source of dead time on a PI file, and most of that dead time is self-inflicted.

Here's the pattern. Somebody orders records early to feel productive. The set comes back covering 3 visits because the client is still treating. It gets paid for, filed, and then re-ordered 4 months later when the demand is being built. The firm paid twice, waited twice, and the second wait is the one sitting between the client and their money.

Ordering early feels like diligence. It's a re-order with extra steps.

The opposite failure costs more. Nobody orders anything until the demand is being assembled, and then a 6-week provider turnaround becomes 6 weeks of nothing happening on a file that was otherwise ready. That delay lands after the case is worth what it's worth, which means it's pure carrying cost.

The trigger is a condition on the file: each provider's treatment status.

> **Order a provider's records when that provider's treatment is complete or has plateaued, and not before.**
>
> Concretely, order when any one is true:
>
> 1. The provider has discharged the client
> 2. The client has stopped attending that provider for 30 days with no scheduled return
> 3. The provider has documented MMI, a permanent impairment rating, or a surgical recommendation you're going to plead
> 4. The provider is about to become unreachable: closing, relocating, or the treating physician is leaving the practice
>
> Order per provider, on that provider's condition. Never wait for the whole file to finish. A file with 5 providers should have records arriving in 5 separate waves, and 4 of them should already be in the folder when the last one clears.
>
> Standing item: bills and records get ordered together, always. Ordering records and chasing bills separately doubles the follow-up work and is the most common reason a demand sits at 95% for 3 weeks.
>
> **Exception:** statute is inside 6 months. Then you order everything immediately regardless of treatment status, accept the incomplete sets, and supplement. A complete record is worth less than a filed case.

Rolling waves instead of one big chase is the whole idea. It converts records from a phase into background work.

## Notes

**Best line is the reframe.** "Ordering early feels like diligence. It's a re-order with extra steps." That's the sentence attorneys will quote back, and it reframes a behavior they currently think of as conscientious.

**Rolling waves is the actual insight.** Most small firms treat records as a single phase that begins after treatment ends. Per-provider triggering compresses the calendar with no added cost.

**Verification status.** The 30-day no-attendance trigger aligns with the industry definition of a treatment gap, which is verified in Ep04's sources. Everything else in the rule is constructed practice. PWB should confirm the 6-month statute exception and whether they'd order bills and records together as an absolute.

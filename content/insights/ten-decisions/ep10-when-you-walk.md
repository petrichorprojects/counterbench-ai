## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

The decision that costs a small PI firm the most money is the one nobody writes down: when to stop working a case you already signed.

A file you should have declined at intake, or dropped at month 2, sits in the drawer for 3 years absorbing paralegal hours, records costs, and the one thing you can't reorder, which is attention. It's the anti-portfolio. Every hour on a case that will net $4,000 is an hour off a case that would net $60,000, and the firm feels busy the entire time it's going backwards.

The reason this stays undecided is that walking feels like failure and signing feels like progress. Both feelings are wrong at the level of the firm.

Here's the uncomfortable version. A firm's real capacity is the number of cases it can work well, and every marginal file past that number degrades the whole book. Saying no to a weak case is how you protect the strong ones. A firm that can't decline is running its best clients at reduced quality to subsidize its worst.

Two moments matter, and they're different decisions.

**Intake.** This is the cheap place to say no and the place firms are worst at it, because an empty pipeline is scary and a signed case feels like money. It isn't, yet.

**Mid-case.** Something changed: liability got worse, the client stopped treating and won't engage, a lien ate the recovery, the client became someone you can't work with. Withdrawal has real constraints, court permission and client notice and timing rules, so this decision has to be made early enough that withdrawal is still clean.

> **Decline or withdraw when 2 of these 3 are true:**
>
> 1. **Expected net to the client** after fees, costs, and liens is below a floor the firm sets in advance and writes down. A file that nets the client less than the effort implies isn't a favor to them.
> 2. **Liability or damages carry a defect** the file can't cure: a causation gap that won't close, a liability dispute you'll lose, a client whose credibility problem is on the record.
> 3. **The client relationship makes the work undoable:** unreachable, dishonest about the facts, or directing the case against your advice in a way that raises your own exposure.
>
> **At intake:** all 3 are cheap to check and the answer costs you nothing but a signature you shouldn't want.
>
> **Mid-case:** the moment 2 are true, the withdrawal analysis starts immediately, because clean withdrawal has a shrinking window and a late one is worse than an early one on every axis, including the client's.
>
> **The hard floor, non-negotiable:** the client's interest governs the withdrawal itself. This rule decides whether the firm recommends parting ways. It never overrides the duty to protect the client through the exit: their file, their statute, their ability to find new counsel in time. You can decide to walk. You cannot walk badly.
>
> **Exception:** a case below the economic floor that the firm takes deliberately, for a reason it names in the file: an existing client, a precedent, a matter of principle. Deliberate and documented is fine. Defaulting into it because nobody looked is the failure.

That's decision 10 of 10.

Here's the thing all 10 have in common. Every one of them is a decision that repeats on every file, has a right answer for your firm, and currently lives in one person's head where it gets applied unevenly and dies when they leave. Writing them down is the cheapest leverage available to a small firm, and almost nobody does it, because writing them down feels like overhead until the day the person who held them is gone.

I've published all 10 over 10 weeks. The compiled version is one page per decision, rule and exceptions, in the form a paralegal executes without asking.

## Notes

**The finale has to close the frame, not just deliver decision 10.** The last two paragraphs pull the whole series back to the Ep00 thesis (knowledge trapped in one head is exposure) and convert the series into the lead-magnet handoff. That's the structural job of this post.

**The anti-portfolio idea is the sharpest in the series.** "The firm feels busy the entire time it's going backwards" is the quotable line and it reframes case-hoarding as the expensive habit it is.

**The withdrawal-ethics floor is mandatory and load-bearing.** This post recommends dropping clients. Without the explicit "you can decide to walk, you cannot walk badly" boundary, it reads as advice to abandon people, which is both wrong and a bar problem. That paragraph is not optional.

**Verification status.** Entirely constructed, and this is the most ethics-sensitive episode because it touches withdrawal duties directly. PWB should confirm the withdrawal framing respects their jurisdiction's rules on permissive withdrawal and client protection, and should set the actual economic floor in condition 1.

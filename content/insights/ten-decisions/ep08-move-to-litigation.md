# Ep08 - Decision 8: What moves a file to litigation

## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

Most files that go into suit at small firms go in because the statute got close, not because anyone decided to file them.

That's a scheduling outcome wearing the costume of a strategic one, and carriers can see it in your filing pattern.

Think about what the adjuster observes across your firm's history. If your suits cluster in the 60 days before statute, they've learned your pre-suit posture is a bluff with a known expiration. That knowledge prices every negotiation you have with that carrier, including on files where you were fully prepared to file.

The decision has 3 real inputs and none of them is the calendar.

**The offer relative to the case's actual value**, from the component sheet in decision 5. The supported value, the one you can decompose, is the only benchmark that counts here. A gap under about 20% is usually a negotiation problem. A gap over 50% is usually a valuation disagreement that won't close by talking.

**Whether the carrier's position is moving.** 3 rounds with movement means keep negotiating. 2 rounds with no movement, or movement under 5%, means they've priced the file and are waiting you out.

**What suit actually changes.** Filing is worth it when it unlocks something specific: discovery you can't get pre-suit, a defendant who behaves differently once represented by appointed counsel, a venue advantage, or a reserve that only moves once litigation costs enter the carrier's math. Filing that unlocks nothing converts a settlement negotiation into a more expensive settlement negotiation.

> **A file moves to litigation when 2 of these 3 are true, and the statute is not one of them:**
>
> 1. The gap between the best offer and the supported case value exceeds 30%
> 2. The carrier has made 2 consecutive rounds with no meaningful movement
> 3. Suit unlocks something identifiable: discovery, a different decision-maker, venue, or reserve movement driven by defense cost
>
> **The hard rule underneath it:** every file gets its litigation decision made no later than 6 months before statute, documented in the file with the reason. A file reaching 6-months-to-statute without that decision recorded gets escalated automatically, and the escalation is a calendar item, not somebody's memory.
>
> **Client consent is separate and comes first.** This rule governs the firm's recommendation. It doesn't govern the client, who may have entirely rational reasons to take less and be finished.
>
> **Exception:** the client's circumstances make litigation delay genuinely harmful. That outweighs all 3 conditions and gets documented as the client's decision.

The 6-month rule is the whole thing. It moves the choice from the deadline to a point where you still have leverage, which is the only difference between filing and being pushed.

That's decision 8 of 10.

## Notes

**The opening accusation is the strongest in the series.** Carriers pattern-match filing behavior across a firm's history. Attorneys know this is true and rarely see it stated, and it reframes an internal scheduling habit as an external pricing problem.

**Numeric thresholds are deliberately debatable.** 20%, 30%, 50%, 5%, 2 rounds. Attorneys will argue with them in the comments, which is the good version of engagement: they're arguing about calibration, which concedes the framework.

**Client consent carve-out is mandatory.** Without it the rule reads as the firm deciding to litigate over a client's head. That's the one objection that would derail this post.

**Verification status.** Entirely constructed. All thresholds need PWB calibration. The 6-month escalation is the load-bearing piece and the one most worth keeping if they cut the percentages.

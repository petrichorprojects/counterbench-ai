## POST

<!-- gate:body -->

Most firms treat the letter of rep as an announcement. The date on it is doing something else entirely.

The duty to preserve evidence attaches when litigation is reasonably anticipated. Your letter is what makes that anticipation a documented fact, on a date, in writing, to a party who now has to explain any gap in what they hand over later.

Which means the LOR is a deadline you set for the other side.

Here's what runs out while a firm waits. Store surveillance overwrites on a fixed cycle, often 7 to 30 days. Vehicles get repaired or totaled and sold. Truck ECM data gets pulled or aged out. Employee witnesses leave. A 911 call log has a retention schedule nobody at your firm has ever looked up.

None of that waits for your intake to finish.

The firms that lose this quietly are the ones that send a clean LOR announcing representation and nothing else, then send a preservation demand 6 weeks later when the file gets a real look. The footage was gone in week 2. There's no sanction available for evidence destroyed before anyone was on notice, and routine destruction before notice is exactly that.

So the letter stops being one letter.

> **The LOR goes out within 72 hours of signed retention, and it never travels alone.**
>
> Every LOR is paired with a preservation demand that names specific categories, not "all relevant evidence":
>
> 1. Video from named locations, with the date range
> 2. The vehicle itself, unrepaired, plus any ECM or telematics data
> 3. Driver qualification and hours-of-service records where a commercial vehicle is involved
> 4. Incident reports, internal communications, and maintenance logs
> 5. Any policy governing retention or automatic deletion of the above
>
> Send to the carrier and to the entity holding the evidence. Those are frequently different, and the carrier has no ability to preserve what a franchisee's DVR is overwriting.
>
> **Exception:** the only reason to delay past 72 hours is an unresolved conflict check. Nothing else qualifies, including an incomplete medical picture. You're preserving liability evidence, and it doesn't depend on knowing the damages yet.

A paralegal sends that. It doesn't need an attorney to originate it, only to sign it.

## Notes

**Why this one lands.** Every attorney reading it has lost footage. The post names the specific mechanism of the loss (routine overwrite before notice attaches) rather than scolding them for being slow.

**The 5-item list is the artifact.** Naming categories instead of "all relevant evidence" is the difference between an enforceable demand and a form letter, and it's the part a paralegal can execute verbatim.

**Verification status.** The preservation mechanism is verified: the duty attaches when litigation is reasonably anticipated, routine deletion is the primary loss vector, and breach can produce sanctions or an adverse inference. The 72-hour window is constructed. PWB should confirm 72 hours is right, and confirm whether the conflict-check exception is the only one they'd accept.

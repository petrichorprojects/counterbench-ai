Medicare lien resolution runs 4 to 8 months end to end. If your firm starts it after settlement, you just told your client their money is 4 to 8 months out, and you told them after they'd already mentally spent it.

That call is the single most common source of client anger at small PI firms, and the case was handled well right up until that moment.

Look at the actual clock. Once the claim is reported to the BCRC, the first conditional payment letter takes about 65 days to issue. That's before any dispute over unrelated charges, which is where the real time goes, because Medicare's itemization routinely includes treatment that has nothing to do with your loss and every line has to be challenged individually.

Post-settlement, the back half is fast. Final demand issues within about 7 to 14 days of reporting the settlement, repayment is due in 60 days before interest accrues, and the closure letter follows in 7 to 12 days. A well-run file wraps the post-settlement cycle in 30 to 45 days.

So the front half is the slow part, and the front half doesn't require a settlement to start. Firms that begin at settlement are choosing to run a 65-day clock in series with a negotiation that's already over.

ERISA is its own problem and it's worse in a specific way. Self-funded ERISA plans carry strong federal reimbursement rights, which means the plan document determines your exposure and you can't estimate it. Firms that guess at an ERISA number in a settlement conversation and then get the plan language are the ones renegotiating a fee to cover the gap.

> **Lien identification starts at intake. Lien resolution starts at demand, never at settlement.**
>
> **At intake:** identify every payer. Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, VA, ERISA or private health, med-pay, workers comp, hospital and provider liens, LOPs. Ask the client directly and confirm against the records, because clients under-report Medicaid and forget med-pay.
>
> **At demand, in parallel with the demand going out:**
> 1. Report to BCRC and start the conditional payment clock
> 2. Request the plan document for any ERISA plan, in writing
> 3. Open negotiations on provider liens and LOPs, which have the most give and the least urgency later
> 4. Dispute unrelated charges on the conditional payment letter line by line as soon as it arrives
>
> **At settlement:** report the settlement, take final demand, pay inside 60 days, get the closure letter.
>
> **The client conversation:** the realistic disbursement timeline gets said out loud at demand time, in writing, before there's a number to be excited about. A client told at demand that Medicare adds months is a client who was informed. The same client told after settlement was surprised, and surprise is what generates the bar complaint.
>
> **Exception:** no government payer and no ERISA plan. Then this compresses to provider liens and LOPs and the timeline is yours to control.

Most of that is paralegal work that requires persistence rather than judgment, which is exactly the work that gets deferred at an understaffed firm until it's on the critical path.

## Notes

**Best verified numbers in the series.** 65 days, 7 to 14 days, 60 days, 4 to 8 months are all real. That makes the core argument arithmetic rather than opinion: the front half is slow, the front half can start early, therefore starting at settlement is a choice.

**The client-conversation clause is the sleeper.** It converts a process post into a bar-complaint-prevention post, which is the CounterbenchAI risk vocabulary. Worth keeping even though it makes the rule longer.

**Strongest retainer tie-in of any episode.** Lien chasing is persistence work, not judgment work, which is precisely the pod model's argument.

**Verification status.** Timelines verified. The intake/demand/settlement staging is constructed. PWB should confirm the intake payer list is complete for their jurisdiction and that opening provider lien negotiations at demand rather than at settlement is right.

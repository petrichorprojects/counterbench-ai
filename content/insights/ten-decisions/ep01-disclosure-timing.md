## POST

Your firm's disclosure rule changes based on who's holding the file that day.

Here's the test. Pull your last 10 files. Did the adjuster learn about the recommended surgery at the same point in all 10?

It didn't happen that way. And the inconsistency has a price.

Carriers want reserves set in concrete within roughly 6 months of the claim opening. State regulators require it and their actuaries price premiums off it.

Flag the recommended surgery inside that window and the reserve moves up. The claim routes to someone with real authority. You're negotiating with a decision-maker instead of a script.

Miss the window and the number is set. Moving it later means the adjuster walks into their supervisor's office, sometimes their supervisor's supervisor's office, to explain why a file they already priced needs a large increase. Some will do that. Most slow-walk it, and your case ages while they decide.

Same decision, opposite outcomes, and at most firms under 10 attorneys it gets made by whoever picked up the file.

A second-year attorney went to Reddit last week to ask how much to tell the adjuster before demand. Read the replies and the real problem shows up. Experienced lawyers, all giving different answers, every one of them correct at their own firm.

There's a firm answer to this question. Yours isn't written down.

Calling that a training problem gets it wrong. Training assumes the knowledge already exists in transferable form. It exists as one senior person's pattern recognition, applied inconsistently, re-derived from scratch by every new hire on live claims with real clients.

That's a documentation problem, and documentation problems are cheap to fix.

Here's the rule we write for firms. Take it.

> **Disclose the recommended procedure when all 3 are true:**
>
> 1. It's in writing from a treating provider. A note saying "may benefit from" doesn't count.
> 2. You have the records supporting the mechanism of injury.
> 3. You're inside roughly 6 months of claim open, or you have a specific reason to believe the reserve is still soft.
>
> If all 3 are true, disclose in writing and ask for the reserve to be reviewed.
>
> If any one is false, disclose nothing beyond the fact of continued treatment.
>
> **Exception:** policy limits are low enough that reserve movement is irrelevant. Then disclose early to trigger the tender conversation.

That's the whole thing. One paragraph. A paralegal applies it without asking anyone.

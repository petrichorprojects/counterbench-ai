## 1. What gets disclosed to the adjuster, and when

Disclose the recommended procedure when all 3 are true:
1. It's in writing from a treating provider. A note saying "may benefit from" doesn't count.
2. You have the records supporting the mechanism of injury.
3. You're inside roughly 6 months of claim open, or you have a specific reason to believe the reserve is still soft.

If all 3 are true, disclose in writing and ask for the reserve to be reviewed. If any one is false, disclose nothing beyond the fact of continued treatment.

**Exception:** policy limits are low enough that reserve movement is irrelevant. Then disclose early to trigger the tender conversation.

*Why it matters: carriers set reserves in concrete within roughly 6 months. Moving a set reserve requires the adjuster to justify an increase up the chain, which they slow-walk.*

## 2. When the letter of rep goes out

The LOR goes out within 72 hours of signed retention, and it never travels alone. Every LOR is paired with a preservation demand naming specific categories, not "all relevant evidence":
1. Video from named locations, with the date range
2. The vehicle itself, unrepaired, plus any ECM or telematics data
3. Driver qualification and hours-of-service records where a commercial vehicle is involved
4. Incident reports, internal communications, maintenance logs
5. Any policy governing retention or automatic deletion of the above

Send to the carrier and to the entity holding the evidence. They are frequently different.

**Exception:** the only reason to delay past 72 hours is an unresolved conflict check. An incomplete medical picture doesn't qualify; you're preserving liability evidence.

*Why it matters: the duty to preserve attaches when litigation is anticipated. Store footage overwrites in 7 to 30 days. Evidence destroyed before notice carries no sanction.*

## 3. What triggers a records chase

Order a provider's records when that provider's treatment is complete or has plateaued, and not before. Order when any one is true:
1. The provider has discharged the client
2. The client has stopped attending for 30 days with no scheduled return
3. The provider has documented MMI, a permanent impairment rating, or a surgical recommendation you'll plead
4. The provider is about to become unreachable

Order per provider, in rolling waves. Bills and records get ordered together, always.

**Exception:** statute is inside 6 months. Order everything immediately, accept incomplete sets, supplement.

*Why it matters: ordering early on an active file means paying and waiting twice. Ordering everything at demand means a 6-week provider turnaround becomes pure carrying cost.*

## 4. When treatment stops and demand starts

Demand goes out when all 3 are true:
1. The client is at MMI, discharged, or documented as plateaued
2. All records and bills are in hand
3. Liens are identified and in process

Separately: every open file gets a days-since-last-treatment check weekly. At 21 days with no scheduled visit, someone calls the client to document why, contemporaneously.

**Exception:** the client is better and done at 3 weeks with a clean discharge. That's a resolved case, not a gap. Demand early.

*Why it matters: a 30-day treatment gap is a red flag on nearly every carrier's screen. A contemporaneous note explaining a gap is worth 10x the same explanation reconstructed at demand.*

## 5. Who sets the demand number, and against what

Every demand number is built from named components, written down before the demand is drafted:
1. Medical specials, split billed vs reasonable-value, with the lien picture noted
2. Wage loss, documented by employer, calculation shown
3. Future care, only when a provider put it in writing
4. General damages, anchored to venue and injury-type verdict data, never a bare multiple of specials
5. The policy and liability reality: limits, comparative fault, collectibility

The attorney sets general damages and the final number. Everything else is assembled by staff and reviewed. The gate: someone who didn't build the number can reconstruct the total from the sheet.

**Exception:** limits are clear and the case is obviously worth them. The number is the limits; the sheet exists to support the bad-faith setup.

*Why it matters: a number you can't decompose is a number you'll negotiate against yourself.*

## 6. When you order the IME rebuttal

Order a paid rebuttal only when all 3 are true:
1. The IME attacks causation, permanency, or mechanism, rather than duration or reasonableness of treatment
2. The treating provider has already been asked for a written response and either declined or didn't cover the disputed point
3. Case value at stake exceeds rebuttal cost by a defensible multiple, and the file is on a litigation track

If any is false, the answer is the treater letter plus cross-examination. When you do order, order immediately on receipt, not at the discovery deadline.

**Exception:** a disclosure deadline forces an earlier order. The deadline wins; note it in the file.

*Why it matters: most defense IMEs concede the injury and attack damages. Those are beaten with the treater's records, not a $3,500 second opinion.*

## 7. What lien work happens pre-demand vs post

Lien identification starts at intake. Lien resolution starts at demand, never at settlement.

At intake: identify every payer (Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, VA, ERISA/private, med-pay, comp, provider liens, LOPs). At demand, in parallel: report to BCRC and start the conditional-payment clock; request ERISA plan documents in writing; open provider-lien and LOP negotiations; dispute unrelated charges line by line. At settlement: report, take final demand, pay inside 60 days.

Say the realistic disbursement timeline out loud at demand, in writing, before there's a number to be excited about.

**Exception:** no government payer and no ERISA plan. Compresses to provider liens and LOPs; the timeline is yours.

*Why it matters: Medicare lien resolution runs 4 to 8 months, and the slow front half doesn't need a settlement to start. Starting at settlement tells the client their money is months out, after they've mentally spent it.*

## 8. What moves a file to litigation

A file moves to litigation when 2 of 3 are true, and the statute is not one of them:
1. The gap between best offer and supported case value exceeds 30%
2. The carrier has made 2 consecutive rounds with no meaningful movement
3. Suit unlocks something identifiable: discovery, a different decision-maker, venue, or reserve movement driven by defense cost

The hard rule underneath: every file gets its litigation decision made no later than 6 months before statute, documented with the reason. Reaching 6-months-to-statute without that decision triggers automatic escalation.

Client consent is separate and comes first; this governs the firm's recommendation, not the client's choice.

**Exception:** the client's circumstances make delay genuinely harmful. Documented as the client's decision.

*Why it matters: suits that cluster in the 60 days before statute teach the carrier your pre-suit posture is a bluff with a known expiration.*

## 9. What the client hears at each stage, and from whom

Every client hears from the firm on a fixed schedule, whether or not anything happened:

| Stage | Cadence | Who |
|---|---|---|
| Intake to treatment | Every 30 days | Paralegal |
| Treatment to demand | Every 30 days + the 21-day gap call | Paralegal |
| Demand pending | Every 21 days | Paralegal |
| Negotiation | Every offer, within 1 business day | Attorney |
| Litigation | Every 30 days + every court date and deposition | Paralegal / attorney |
| Settlement to disbursement | Every 14 days until the check clears | Paralegal |

Attorney-only: any offer, recommendation, change in value, bad development, or legal advice. Staff never delivers a number the client must react to. Everything gets logged.

**Exception:** a client who asks for less contact gets it in writing, and still receives the attorney contacts.

*Why it matters: the ABA lists neglect and lack of communication as the two most common disciplinary complaints against lawyers, and Model Rule 1.4 among the rules producing the most discipline. Silence during a slow phase gets filled with the worst available explanation.*

## 10. When you walk

Decline or withdraw when 2 of 3 are true:
1. Expected net to the client, after fees, costs, and liens, is below a floor the firm sets in advance and writes down
2. Liability or damages carry a defect the file can't cure
3. The client relationship makes the work undoable: unreachable, dishonest, or directing the case against advice in a way that raises the firm's exposure

At intake, all 3 are cheap to check. Mid-case, the moment 2 are true, the withdrawal analysis starts immediately, because clean withdrawal has a shrinking window.

The hard floor: the client's interest governs the withdrawal itself. You can decide to walk. You cannot walk badly. The ABA lists Model Rule 1.16(d), declining or terminating representation, among the rules producing the most discipline.

**Exception:** a below-floor case taken deliberately for a named reason (existing client, precedent, principle). Deliberate and documented is fine. Defaulting into it because nobody looked is the failure.

*Why it matters: a case you should have declined absorbs the attention that would have netted a strong case. The firm feels busy the whole time it's going backwards.*
